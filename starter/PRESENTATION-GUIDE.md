# PRESENTATION GUIDE — Your Loom Walkthrough

Your Loom is where you convince us there's a thinking engineer behind the code.
A demo that just clicks through screens saying "here's the home page, here's the
detail page" scores poorly. We can see the screens. We want to hear the reasoning.

**Length: 5 minutes maximum.** This is a hard cap, not a target. A tight,
well-edited 4 minutes beats a rambling 5. Part of the skill being tested is
saying the important things and cutting the rest. If you go over 5 minutes, we
stop watching at 5 — so put your best thinking first.

Record your screen with your voice. Show your face if you can (Loom makes this
easy) — presenting your work is a real skill and part of the job.

---

## Five minutes is short — spend it on thinking, not touring

You cannot show everything in 5 minutes, and you're not meant to. The marks are
for judgment, so weight your time toward the decisions. A rough budget:

- ~30s — the pitch
- ~2 min — your hardest decision + where you pushed back on Thabo (the core)
- ~1.5 min — a fast live demo of the thing actually working
- ~1 min — one piece of code you own + your AI moment

If you have to cut something, cut the demo tour, not the reasoning. We can open
the app ourselves; we cannot read your mind.

## What to cover, in order

### 1. The 30-second pitch (start here)
Pretend we are Thabo and two investors. In two or three sentences: what did you
build, who is it for, and what is the one thing it does well? If you can't say it
crisply, your scope was probably too wide.

### 2. The hardest decision you made (spend real time here)
Pick the single most interesting call from your Decision Log and walk us through
it. What was the situation, what were the options, what did you choose, what did
you give up. This is the most important 90 seconds of the whole video. We are
listening for genuine tradeoff reasoning, not "I picked React because it's
popular."

### 3. Where you pushed back on Thabo
Show us one thing he asked for that you refused, reshaped, or cut. Explain the
call the way you would to his face. This is where critical thinking shows.

### 4. A live demo of the thing working
Now show the app. Drive it like a real user: search for something, filter it,
open an item, go through the booking flow. **Something will feel rough — narrate
it honestly.** "This is where I ran out of time, here's what I'd do next" scores
better than pretending it's perfect. Do a quick pass on your phone or a narrow
window to prove it's responsive.

### 5. One piece of code you're proud of, and why
Open the editor. Show one type, component, or piece of state you think is well
built, and explain what makes it good. Then — and this matters — show one thing
you would refactor if you had another week, and why. Knowing what's weak in your
own code is a senior trait.

### 6. Your AI moment
Talk us through one time you used AI and it was wrong, or gave you something you
had to reshape. What did it miss? How did you catch it? This directly demonstrates
the judgment the whole assessment is built around.

### 7. The curveball (if one was issued to you — see below)
If you received a founder curveball at submission, show how you'd absorb it and
whether your architecture makes it easy or hard. You don't have to have built it
— talk through where it would go and what it would touch.

---

## The founder curveball

At or near submission, some of you will receive a short "Thabo just messaged"
change request (for example: "actually can items be rented by the hour not the
day" or "investors want it in two languages"). This tests whether your code can
bend. You are not required to fully implement it — you are required to show, in
the Loom or a short note, how much of your app it touches and why that is or isn't
easy. **An architecture that makes change cheap is the whole game.** If you built
everything hard-coded, you'll feel this. That feeling is the lesson.

---

## How the Loom is marked

- **Clarity of thinking** — can you explain a decision cleanly, out loud, without
  hiding behind jargon.
- **Honesty** — you name what's rough, what's unfinished, what you'd change. We
  trust engineers who can see their own gaps.
- **Ownership** — it's clear you understand every part of what you're showing,
  including anything AI helped with. If you can't explain a line, don't claim it.
- **Communication** — you'd be comfortable saying this to a real founder and real
  investors.

## Things that quietly lose you marks
- Reading your Decision Log aloud word for word. Talk to us instead.
- A silent demo with no reasoning.
- Claiming everything is perfect. Nothing is. We know.
- Showing code you clearly don't understand.
- Going over 5 minutes. Respect for the listener's time is a professional skill,
  and editing yourself is part of what's being marked.

## One last thing
The best Looms feel like a confident engineer giving a founder a straight,
honest update: here's what I built, here's what I chose not to, here's why,
here's what's next. Aim for that.
